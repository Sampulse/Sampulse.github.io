Through the Flood – Adaeze’s Rescue from a Life of Secrets and Storms

Adaeze Nwankwo was known in Enugu for her charm. Smart, beautiful, always put-together, and deeply involved in church, no one would guess the torrents she was swimming through inside. Her laugh echoed through youth fellowships, and her voice led many worship sessions. Yet beneath the surface of this seemingly perfect Christian young woman was a storm few knew existed.

Shadows in the Sanctuary

Adaeze grew up in a strict Christian household. Her father was a deacon, her mother the choir director. Every Sunday, the family sat in the front pew. Expectations were high—there was little room for failure. From a young age, Adaeze learned how to perform holiness, how to wear the perfect dress, quote scriptures fluently, and hide her questions.

When she was 12, an older cousin moved in temporarily. What began as innocent companionship turned into a nightmare of repeated abuse. Adaeze, too afraid to speak, buried the pain under hymns and memory verses. The silence wrapped her heart in shame and secrecy, convincing her that brokenness had no place in the temple.

She carried that wound into adulthood. By the time she reached university, she had mastered the art of smiling while drowning. She led prayer groups and preached in fellowships, yet went home each night to tears and nightmares. The trauma festered in the silence, and faith became a mask she wore, not a refuge she ran to.

Floodwaters Rise

It wasn’t until Adaeze started seeing Chuka, a final-year Law student, that the storm surged. Chuka was attentive and godly. For the first time, she felt seen. But when he proposed after graduation, Adaeze panicked. Every part of her past rose like a flood — the shame, the hidden abuse, the self-hatred.

She withdrew. Ghosted calls. Stopped attending church. Locked herself in her hostel room for days. The fear of being known — truly known — was more terrifying than loneliness.

Her roommates tried praying. One even suggested deliverance. But Adaeze didn’t need exorcism. She needed healing — soul-deep restoration. She needed someone to look at her and say, “You’re not too broken for God.”

The Collapse

One rainy afternoon, everything caved in. The pain, the silence, the loneliness — it became too heavy. Adaeze stood on the bridge just outside campus, eyes locked on the current below. She thought, "Maybe if I just disappeared, the shame would go with me." But before she could move, a hand grabbed her wrist. It was Chuka.

He didn’t yell. He didn’t preach. He just held her as she wept for over an hour. For the first time, she told him everything. And instead of walking away, Chuka whispered, “You are not what happened to you. You are beloved.”

The Rescue

With Chuka’s encouragement, Adaeze started therapy. It wasn’t quick or easy. There were relapses, panic attacks, and days she didn’t want to speak. But slowly, the layers peeled. She also met a Christian counselor who helped her reframe Scripture not as a set of rules but as a healing balm.

One verse that stayed with her was Isaiah 43:2 — "When you pass through the waters, I will be with you..." That verse became her anchor. She wasn’t alone in the flood. God was with her in it, not just waiting on the other side.

Adaeze returned to church, but not to perform. This time, she testified. She spoke openly about her healing journey. At first, some leaders were uncomfortable — vulnerability wasn’t common on the pulpit. But her honesty began to draw others out. Survivors. Doubters. People who wore masks just like she used to.

Purpose from Pain

After graduation, Adaeze launched “Floodlight,” a faith-based initiative that offered free counseling and support groups for sexual abuse survivors in universities across southeastern Nigeria. What began with 10 students in a rented classroom grew into a regional movement.

She was invited to speak at youth conferences, sharing her story not as a victim but a victor. “God didn’t cause my pain,” she’d say, “but He refused to let it be wasted.”

Today, Adaeze is a certified trauma counselor. She and Chuka are married and serve together, traveling across West Africa advocating for faith-informed mental health care. Her story — once hidden beneath shame — now shines like a beacon to others still trapped in storms.

“Through the flood, He was with me. I didn’t drown. I discovered I could walk on water.”

This is Adaeze’s story — of secrets, storms, and sacred healing. A testament that God doesn’t just rescue from the flood — He walks with us through it, rewriting broken chapters into testimonies.
